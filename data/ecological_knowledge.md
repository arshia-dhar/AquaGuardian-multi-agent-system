# Coral Reef Ecology

## Reef Importance

Coral reefs occupy less than 1% of the ocean floor but support approximately 25% of all marine life.

Healthy reefs provide:

- Habitat for thousands of marine species
- Coastal protection
- Fisheries resources
- Tourism value

---

## Indicators of Healthy Reefs

Typical indicators include:

- High fish diversity
- Clear water
- Live coral cover
- Minimal algal growth
- Presence of apex predators
- Healthy herbivorous fish populations

---

## Signs of Reef Degradation

Potential warning signs include:

- Coral bleaching
- Marine debris
- Excessive algae
- Sediment accumulation
- Low fish diversity
- Broken coral colonies

---

## Important Marine Species

Butterflyfish

Often used as indicators of reef health because they depend heavily on live coral.

Parrotfish

Control algal growth and contribute to coral reef resilience.

Surgeonfish

Prevent algae from overwhelming coral reefs.

Sea Turtles

Maintain healthy seagrass ecosystems and contribute to marine biodiversity.

Sharks

Apex predators that regulate marine food webs.

Manta Rays

Large filter feeders indicating productive plankton ecosystems.

Dolphins

Highly intelligent marine mammals commonly associated with productive coastal waters.

---

## Marine Hazards

Hazards include:

- Plastic pollution
- Ghost fishing nets
- Oil contamination
- Sediment plumes
- Coral bleaching
- Shipwrecks
- Underwater archaeological structures

---

## AUV Mission Recommendations

If reef conditions appear healthy:

- Continue routine monitoring.
- Record biodiversity changes.
- Archive imagery.

If degradation is detected:

- Recommend additional surveys.
- Compare against previous missions.
- Notify marine researchers.

If protected species are observed:

- Maintain safe operating distances.
- Avoid unnecessary disturbance.
- Document observations for conservation efforts.
